DIYskarte

(on hiring)

Toffer Lorenzana

The Problem

Most founders hire before they understand the job.


They don’t know what good output looks like. They can’t describe the role clearly enough to evaluate someone in it. So they post the job, find someone, hand it off — and two weeks later they’re doing it themselves again.


The problem wasn’t the person. The job was never clearly defined. And the only way to define it clearly is to do it yourself first.

You can’t teach what you haven’t done. And you can’t hire well for a job you don’t understand.

The Framework

D-0: DO IT YOURSELF


Before anyone else comes in, you do the work.


You learn what the role actually takes — the decisions that aren’t obvious, the steps that look simple until you’re doing them. You build a sense of what good output feels like.


Founders who skip this build a process around a misunderstanding they don’t know they have.


The ones who go through the work first know what they’re evaluating when someone does it in front of them. That knowledge is hard to fake.

BOOTSTRAPPING IS KNOWING


Founders who’ve had to do every job before delegating it develop sharper instincts than founders who hired early. They know what good looks like because they’ve produced it themselves. That’s where pattern recognition comes from — not talent, but firsthand experience.

Document and Demonstrate.

D-1: DOCUMENT


Once you can do the job consistently, write it down as a checklist.


Every meaningful step, in order, in language clear enough for someone new to follow without asking. A way to check output quality at each stage.


It will have gaps the first time. What matters is that someone other than you can actually use it.


D-2: DEMONSTRATE


When most founders sit down to walk someone through the checklist, they discover they don’t follow it themselves.


They’ve been doing the job on instinct, and the checklist only captured part of it. Steps are missing. Decisions happen that were never written down.


Follow the checklist yourself, out loud, in front of someone. Every time you deviate, stop and update it. Keep going until what you do and what’s written are the same. Only then is it ready to hand off.

The Demonstrate step isn’t for the person you’re training. It’s for you. It’s where you find the gaps in your own process.

D-3: DUPLICATE


Now the new person runs the checklist in front of you.


Watch for where they get stuck, where they read steps differently than you intended, where the gaps still are.


The goal is calibration — is the checklist complete, can this person follow it consistently, does their output meet your standard. When all three are true, the process owns the role.

DIY Addiction

Doing the job yourself has a side effect nobody talks about. The better you get, the higher your standard becomes — and the harder it is to accept someone else doing the work.


So you keep doing it yourself, because no one ever feels quite good enough. You trained them. You handed it off. And then you quietly took it back.


It takes one of three forms.


  • The competence trap: the more skilled you become, the more flaws you see, the bar keeps rising, and no hire ever clears it.
  • The identity trap: being the best at the thing is part of who you are — handing it off is personal, and that’s harder than any process can fix.
  • The perfectionism loop: you keep improving as you go, the standard never settles, and the hire never happens.

D-4: DETACH


Detach means accepting one thing: 70% done by someone else, consistently and without you, is worth more than 100% done by you indefinitely.


The goal was always to build a process good enough to run without you.


You do it before you feel ready, because you won’t feel ready. A business that still requires you to do every job you’ve already documented and trained someone else to do has a ceiling.


That ceiling is you.

THE CORE OF IT


Most early hires go wrong because the role was never clearly defined before someone was put in it.


The first three Ds fix that by forcing you to define it through doing it.

THE HARDEST PART


Most founders who can’t scale don’t fail at the first three Ds. They fail at the fourth. They document, demonstrate, duplicate — and then quietly take the job back. The hire didn’t fail. Letting go felt like losing.

How to Use This

Whenever you’re putting someone into a role you built yourself:


  1. D-0: Do it yourself until you can produce a consistent, quality result.
  2. D-1: Document every step in a checklist detailed enough for someone new to follow without asking.
  3. D-2: Demonstrate — follow the checklist yourself in real time until what’s written matches what you actually do.
  4. D-3: Duplicate — watch your hire run the checklist and calibrate until the output meets the standard.
  5. D-4: Detach — let the process run. Your job now is to protect the standard.

The result is a documented, repeatable process — a business that runs when you step back.
